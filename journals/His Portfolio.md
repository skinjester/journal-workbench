# GDC Talk

From the start, I designed around a mismatch: live video ran at 30 fps, but hallucinations updated only 3–5 times per second. A fully resolved DeepDream could take 20 seconds to appear, so I traded spatial resolution for temporal resolution. The magic was in watching the image emerge from noise — that’s what had drawn me to this work in the first place.

If the subject in front of the camera was still (beneath the motion-detection threshold), the system showed the evolving dream. Movement switched it back to live video, creating a hide-and-seek between viewer and machine. Smooth transitions were critical: lower frame rates were fine, but delays in showing movement felt like the system had frozen. This challenge became the driving force behind my technical work, leading to a complete rethink of the pipeline and the multithreaded architecture that powered it.

### **DeepDreamVisionQuest at GDC**

- **Project**: _DeepDreamVisionQuest_ (2016–2019)
    
- **Context**: Premiered at GDC as an interactive installation.
    
- **Technology**: Built using Python, OpenCV, and Google's Inception-v3 model (DeepDream algorithm).
    
- **Experience**: The system "hallucinates" live video, transforming camera feeds into evolving psychedelic visuals driven by AI misunderstanding.
    
- **Concept**: Framed as _"multiplayer hallucinations"_ or _"encounters with a gregarious learning machine."_
    
- **Interactive Layer**: Participants interact with a neural network that either mirrors or windows the world, depending on the camera direction.
    
- **Themes**: Ritual interface, machine perception, collective dream logic, and AI misrecognition.

This project started when I saw strange, dreamlike pictures created by Google’s DeepDream algorithm. What fascinated me wasn’t just how the images looked, but how they slowly came into focus over time—like watching a dream form out of nothing. I wondered: _What if those dreams could come from a live camera instead?_

To find out, I started experimenting. I used Python, OpenCV, and Caffe to build a system that showed the world to a neural network. DeepDream doesn’t work in real time, so I came up with a trick: the system would only "dream" when nothing was moving. As soon as it saw motion, the dream would stop and reset. That way, people could shape what the machine saw just by standing still or moving.

Figuring out motion detection became the heart of the project. I didn’t use AI for that part—I used simple tools that counted how many pixels changed between frames. I tested lots of methods from online tutorials, even if I didn’t fully understand the math. I wrote small programs to compare different approaches and slowly improved the system. In the end, I added a simple control panel so I could adjust the sensitivity using a keyboard or game controller. This helped the system work better in different lighting or noisy environments.

I showed the project at the 2016 Game Developers Conference as a live demo and interactive poster session. The system turned a live video feed into strange, evolving images that reacted to how people moved. Visitors stood in front of the camera and watched the machine hallucinate around them. After GDC, the project grew. I turned it into a platform called **DeepDream Vision Quest** and showed it at other events like the LAST Festival, CODAME, and UC Berkeley. It also became the basis for a series of workshops I taught on creative AI and generative art.

# Design Brief


Here is what I want to build in Plasmic Studio. I've included a diagram  
  
TabMenu  
A horizontal container holding a sequence of TabItems (Menu-1, Menu-2, …).  
  
TabItem  
An interactive Tab component instance. Exactly one TabItem is “selected” at any time.  
  
ContentSlider  
A carousel/slider beneath the tab bar. It has one variant per TabItem; when Tab N is selected, the slider shows its _N_th panel.  
  
Viewport  
The TabMenu lives inside a fixed‐width viewport (clipped by a mask).  
  
When the viewport is wide enough, all TabItems are visible; otherwise they overflow and the user scrolls the rail to see the hidden ones.  
  
Interaction Modes  
1. Click-to-Select  
Tap on any TabItem.  
  
If it’s already fully in view, simply select it and switch the ContentSlider variant.  
  
If it’s off‐screen (or only partly visible), auto-scroll the rail just far enough to bring that tab fully into view, then select it and switch the slider.  
  
2. Scroll-to-Select  
Swipe/drag the TabMenu rail horizontally.  
  
Determine the direction of the swipe (left or right) and identify the adjacent TabItem in that direction.  
  
Adjacent tab fully visible?  
  
Yes → Immediately mark that tab as selected (no further scrolling), then switch the ContentSlider.  
  
No → Smoothly scroll the rail until the adjacent tab becomes fully visible. As soon as it is fully in view, mark it selected and switch the slider.
The user should feel like:  
- They can manually override with a tap.  
- The UI stays in sync with scroll position when navigating naturally.

## What strategies do digital product designers use when designing innovative products or experiences that have no existing precedent?

- **Cross-disciplinary Teams**: Combine expertise from design, engineering, psychology, and even art to generate unique perspectives.
    
- **Open Brainstorming**: Use techniques like “provocation,” where wild ideas are encouraged to spark fresh thinking.
    

### 4. **Design for Future Scalability**

- **Think Modular**: Create components or frameworks that allow for flexible future adaptations.
    
- **Future-Proofing**: Anticipate technological advancements or changing user needs to design products that can evolve.
    

### 5. **Leverage Emerging Technologies**

- **Use AI or Data Analytics**: Generate insights from trends, user data, and predictive modeling.
    
- **Experiment with Tech like AR/VR**: Explore immersive technologies to redefine user experiences.
    

### 6. **Storytelling Approach**

- **Narrative Frameworks**: Build the product journey as a story, focusing on the emotions and experiences of the user.
    
- **Visual Storyboards**: Map out the journey visually, making abstract ideas tangible.
    

This process often requires patience and an open mind, as creating something novel means navigating uncertainties.

## What kind of products do product designers design?

Product designers work across a spectrum of products, often tailoring their approach based on whether they’re operating in the digital or physical realm. Here’s a deeper dive into the kinds of products they design:

### 1. **Digital Products**
   - **Mobile Apps & Web Applications:**  
     Designers create intuitive interfaces and seamless user experiences for apps and websites ranging from e-commerce and social media platforms to productivity tools and enterprise solutions.
   
   - **Software and SaaS Platforms:**  
     These include everything from business software, financial services tools, health applications, to educational platforms. Designers ensure that functionality, clarity, and engagement are balanced with a compelling visual experience.
   
   - **User Interfaces (UI) & Experiences (UX):**  
     Beyond applications, product designers craft the detailed user experience for digital ecosystems. This may involve designing dashboards, control panels, or interactive features that facilitate smooth user journeys.

### 2. **Physical Products and Hybrid Experiences**
   - **Consumer Electronics and Smart Devices:**  
     In this role, designers often shape the look, feel, and functionality of items like smartphones, wearables, or home automation devices. The challenge lies in integrating form and function while considering manufacturing constraints.
   
   - **Internet of Things (IoT) and Connected Products:**  
     These products fuse digital and physical elements—think smart thermostats or connected appliances. Designers work on both the physical interactions (buttons, displays) and the complementary digital experience (mobile app control, user feedback loops).

### 3. **Service-Driven and Experiential Products**
   - **Integrated Ecosystems:**  
     Here, the focus is on the end-to-end experience. Designers might map out entire customer journeys, ensuring that all touchpoints—from a website to in-store interactions—deliver a cohesive experience.
   
   - **Immersive and Emerging Experiences:**  
     With the rise of AR/VR and other immersive technologies, product designers are increasingly challenged to craft digital experiences that feel tangible and engaging, even when there’s no traditional “interface” to work with.

### 4. **Specialized and Niche Products**
   - **Games and Interactive Media:**  
     Designing interactive experiences or gaming interfaces involves honing in on engaging storytelling, intuitive controls, and memorable aesthetics.

## what is the difference betwen a UX designer and a Product designer

While the terms often overlap and can vary by company, here’s a breakdown of the core differences and overlaps between a **UX Designer** and a **Product Designer**:

### **Scope and Focus**

- **UX Designer:**
    
    - **Primary Focus:** Concentrates on the user’s internal experience. This includes making interfaces intuitive, ensuring smooth interactions, and optimizing navigation.
        
    - **Key Tasks:** Conducting user research, creating wireframes and prototypes, usability testing, and refining flows based on feedback.
        
    - **Goal:** Enhance the usability and accessibility of a product—ensuring that every interaction is clear, efficient, and satisfying for the user.
        
- **Product Designer:**
    
    - **Primary Focus:** Takes a broader view that encompasses not just the user experience, but also the product’s overall vision, market positioning, and business goals.
        
    - **Key Tasks:** In addition to usability and interface design, product designers are often involved in strategic decision-making, visual design, and even aspects of product management. They consider business requirements, market trends, and technological feasibility.
        
    - **Goal:** Deliver a product that is not only delightful to use but also aligns well with brand identity, solves a real problem, and is viable in the market.
        

### **Contextual Examples**

- **UX Designer in Action:** When revamping a mobile banking app, a UX designer might focus on simplifying the login process, ensuring that account information is easy to access, and testing various micro-interactions (like button feedback) to reduce user error.
    
- **Product Designer in Action:** For the same mobile banking app, a product designer would consider the overall customer journey—from onboarding to long-term engagement. They might decide on new features based on market trends, ensure the look aligns with the bank’s brand, and collaborate with business teams on how the app can support financial goals (like encouraging savings or investments).
    

### **Why the Distinction Matters**

- The **UX Designer** is pivotal for ensuring that the product is tailored to the needs and behaviors of its users. Their success is measured by improvements in task success rates, reduced friction, and overall satisfaction.
    
- The **Product Designer** bridges the gap between user-centric design and strategic business objectives. They ensure that the design process not only results in an engaging product experience but also contributes to the product’s commercial success and scalability.
    

This distinction illustrates that while all product designers have a strong foundation in UX principles, the role expands to include a wider mix of strategy, visual design, and product management—which is crucial for delivering a product that excels on all fronts.

In many teams, the product designer sets the overall vision, ensuring that every touchpoint—from wireframe to final release—is both user-friendly and strategically sound, while the UX designer dives deep into perfecting the details of the interaction.

### **Digging Deeper**

- **Overlap and Collaboration:** In lean or startup environments, the roles might merge and evolve fluidly based on project needs. A product designer might need to wear the UX hat often and vice versa. The key is that both roles aim to enhance the experience, just from slightly different perspectives.
    
- **Career Paths:** Those starting in UX might gravitate toward a product design role as they gain a broader understanding of business and strategy, while some product designers might focus more deeply on user psychology and behavior.
    

This dynamic interplay highlights why both perspectives are crucial in creating products that not only delight users but also succeed in the competitive market.

## What makes it hard for product managers to evaluate a prospective designer when hiring new talent?

Hiring a designer isn’t just hiring a set of skills—it’s hiring a _thinking partner under pressure_. The problem is, most hiring processes aren’t designed to evaluate that.

### **1. Design Portfolios Don’t Always Tell the Whole Story**

Most portfolios show the final pixels. Maybe a figma file. Maybe some user flows. But what PMs _really_ want to know is:

- Did this person help resolve ambiguity?
- Were they a force for alignment?
- Did they push the product forward—or just make it prettier?
    
🧠 _Why it’s hard_: Good storytelling can mask weak collaboration. And good design can be derailed by bad dynamics—so it’s tough to tell how much of the success was the designer’s doing.

### **2. Collaboration Is Invisible on Paper**

Design is inherently collaborative—but resumes and case studies are individualistic. PMs are trying to figure out:

- Can this person take feedback without falling apart?
- Can they advocate for users _and_ adjust when engineering says “no”?
- Will they speak up in a roadmap meeting—or just wait to be told?
    
🤝 _Why it’s hard_: There’s no “team dynamic” section in a portfolio. And “plays well with others” is easy to fake in interviews.

### **3. Craft Can Obscure Strategy (and Vice Versa)**

Some designers show dazzling visuals. Others show dense flows and data dashboards. But PMs often struggle to find the balance:

- Does this designer understand business objectives?
- Do they _know_ when to push for polish and when to ship the simplest version?

📉 _Why it’s hard_: PMs want someone who can scale up or down, but most portfolios skew one way. The real value is in range and judgment—which are hard to quantify.

### **4. "Fit" Is Fuzzy and Overloaded**

“Culture fit.” “Startup experience.” “Can they work fast?” PMs are looking for someone who matches the _cadence_ of their team—but they often conflate that with background or buzzwords.

🧩 _Why it’s hard_: The best designer for a team might be the _opposite_ of everyone already there. But hiring bias leans toward the familiar.

### **5. Great Designers Ask Hard Questions**

The best designers challenge the brief, dig into the “why,” and sometimes tell PMs uncomfortable truths. That can feel like friction—until it becomes the reason the product succeeds.

🔥 _Why it’s hard_: In an interview, it’s tough to distinguish a true collaborator from someone who’ll fight every decision. And PMs are often afraid to hire someone who’ll push _too_ hard.

### Challenges Software Engineers Face When Working with Designers

#### Communication Barriers
- **Different vocabularies**: Engineers speak in technical terms while designers use visual language
- Misinterpretation of requirements and intentions
- Difficulty translating design concepts into technical specifications
#### Conflicting Priorities
- Engineers focus on functionality, performance, and code quality
- Designers prioritize user experience, aesthetics, and brand consistency
- Balancing technical feasibility with design vision
#### Technical Constraints
- Design concepts that are difficult or impractical to implement
- Platform limitations not considered in design
- Performance implications of complex design elements
#### Workflow Misalignment
- Different tools and methodologies
- Asynchronous work patterns
- Handoff friction when transitioning from design to development
#### Implementation Challenges
- Pixel-perfect implementation expectations
- Responsive design complexities
- Accessibility requirements that may conflict with visual design
#### Feedback Cycles
- Iterative changes that require significant code refactoring
- Late-stage design modifications
- Difficulty communicating technical debt created by design changes
#### Documentation Issues
- Incomplete design specifications
- Missing interaction states or edge cases
- Inconsistent component definitions

What is kanban?
In Japanese the word "kanban" means sign or billboard. Its a visual signal that's used to trigger an action. For example, an empty store shelf signals that merchandise needs to be restocked

When adapted for Agile development, kanban is a method for developing products with an emphasis on just-in-time delivery and the optimization of the flow of work on the team. Typically includes these three categories:

Queue: work that needs to be done
In Progress: work that has been retrieved from the queue and is currently being worked on
Done: completed work

each category limits the nuber of work items that are assigned to it


###### 2021-01-24 12:39
Differences between Kanban & Scrum

TIMIMG
- Scrum
  + In scrum methodology teams prioritize workd and commit to a certain number of tasks within a sprint. Work is released at the end of each sprint
- Kanban
  + Kanban is not limited to an iteration or sprint cycle. It supports continuous delivery while maximizing

WORK IN PROGRESS
- Scrum
  + Tasks cannot be added to the scrum board in the middle of the sprint, but it is possible for all tasks to be in the In Progress column at the same time.
- Kanban
  + The team agrees on the number of tasks that can be in progress at the same time. As tasks are completed, team members pull new tasks from a queue.

CHANGE
- Scrum
  + Work is not added during a sprint
- Kanban
  + Work can be added at any time

A few templates for crafting a value proposition

# VALUE POSITIONING
For _target customer_
who _statement of the need or opportunity_
our _product/service_ is _product category_
that _statement of benefit_

example:
For _non technical marketers_ who _struggle to find return on investment in social media_ our _product_ is a _web-based analytics software_ that _translates engagement metrics into actionable revenue metrics_


# HIGH CONCEPT PITCH
_Proven industry example_ for/of _new domain_

examples:
FLickr for video
The Firefox of media players


# XYZ
We help _X_ do _Y_ doing _Z_

example:
We help _non-technical marketers_  _discover return on inventment in social media_ by _turning engagement metrics into revenue metrics_


# CUSTOMER, PROBLEM, SOLUTION
Customer _who your customer is_
Problem _what problem you're solving for the customer_
Solution _what is your solution for the problem_

example:
_Small and medium sized business markets_ _cannot easily measure campaign ROI because existing solutions are too expensive, complicated to deploy, and display a dizzying array of non-actionable charts._ _I provide a Low cost, easy to deploy analytics system designed for non-technical marketers who need actionable metrics._


# ELEVATOR RIDE
- Short, simple and memorable: _what, how, why_
- three keywords or phrases
- no expert jargon, so an 8yr old can understand it too

example:
_mint.com_ is the _free, easy way_ to _manage your money online_


# PITCHCRAFT
- Highlight the enormity of the problem you are tackling.
- Tell the audience up front what your company sells.
- Distill the differentiation down to one, easy-to-comprehend sentence.
- Establish credibility by sharing the pedigree of the entrepreneurs, customers, or the investors.

example:
_One person dies of melanoma every 62 minutes_ We offer a _dermatoscope app for iPhone_ that _enables people to easily diagnose their skin, leveraging patented pattern recognition technology_ trusted by the _World Health Organization_


# ICEBERG
Superlative _why choose this product_
Label _what is this product_
Qualifiers _who should choose this product_

example:
The _easiest_ _operating system_ for _netbook PC's_
The _most secure_ _payment gateway_ for _mobile e-commerce

# WHY
Why:
How:
What:

example:
In everything we do _we believe in challenging the status quo._ We believe in thinking differently. The way we challenge the status quo is _making our products beautifully designed, simple to use and user friendly_. _We just happen to make computers_

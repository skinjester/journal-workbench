## PART 1 — What the role is really asking for

#### The explicit asks

This is not a generic product UX role. World wants someone who can own a trust-critical device journey end to end: the person being scanned, the operator running the session, the physical setup around the device, and the digital surfaces attached to that flow.

The written requirements cluster around five things: multi-touchpoint systems thinking, hardware-plus-software collaboration, user research, concept-to-launch ownership, and post-launch iteration in field conditions. The role is also unusually explicit about multi-sensory design: audio, lighting, haptics, physical feedback, and instructional cues are part of the product surface.

#### The implied asks

The team is likely trying to reduce failure, confusion, and distrust in a sensitive biometric workflow. "Seamless" here probably means fewer operator interventions, fewer drop-offs during verification, clearer recovery when scans fail, and a setup that feels safe rather than uncanny.

The industrial-design-team placement matters. They are not only asking for interface screens; they want a designer who can translate intent across ID, hardware, firmware, software, operations, and manufacturing without losing the experience thread.

#### What "great" looks like for this role

Great for this job probably looks like someone who has already shipped hardware-adjacent or device-led experiences where physical placement, device feedback, and operator behavior all affect success. The strongest candidate would show recent proof of research-led iteration, service-flow thinking, and specification work that survives real-world deployment.

#### What a hiring team is optimizing for in screening

- Fast recognition that this person can work beyond app screens.
- Proof of systems thinking across physical and digital touchpoints.
- Confidence that they can operate in ambiguity without losing rigor.
- Signals that they can partner with engineering, ID, and operations rather than staying in a pure visual or pure product lane.
- Evidence that trust, clarity, and behavioral guidance matter in their work.

#### Likely doubts unless proven otherwise

- Has he actually led UX for a real hardware device ecosystem, not just software and interactive installations?
- Can he design for an operator workflow and a participant workflow in the same service system?
- Are audio, lighting, haptics, and environmental cues areas of direct authorship or just adjacent exposure?
- Is the strongest proof recent enough, and is enough of it outside games and creative technology?

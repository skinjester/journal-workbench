## PART 1 — What the role is really asking for

#### The explicit asks
This is a senior UX role for a game studio shipping player-facing systems. The job wants someone who can own UX from requirements through wireframes, flows, prototypes, and implementation handoff for gameplay systems, menus, and commerce.

The obvious asks are:
- translate vague feature requirements into usable interaction structure
- build interactive Figma prototypes and component-ready wireframes
- make gamepad and mouse/keyboard UX feel coherent
- create IA that engineers can implement and artists can polish
- work closely with design, UI art, engineering, QA, and production
- iterate based on playtests, implementation constraints, and console realities

#### The implied asks
The implied ask is not "draw better menus." It is "reduce product risk inside a fast-moving game production pipeline."

That means the studio likely wants someone who can:
- turn messy gameplay ideas into shippable structure
- keep a live-service product legible as it grows
- defend UX decisions when implementation, cert, and gameplay needs collide
- anticipate edge cases in controller navigation, shop flows, and dense RPG systems
- be hands-on enough to stay close to the work, not just set direction

There is also a hidden bar here around ownership. They do not just want a collaborator. They want someone who can take a feature, stabilize the UX logic, and keep it from turning into a pile of exceptions.

#### What "great" looks like for this role
Great for this role looks like a designer who can do four things well at once:
- understand game systems at the feature level
- make those systems usable on controller and keyboard/mouse
- package the work in a way engineers and producers can actually execute
- keep the UX coherent as implementation realities force changes

For Eleventh Hour specifically, "great" likely also means:
- comfort with RPG complexity and commerce
- a bias toward clarity over ornament
- enough experience on shipped games that you do not need the basics explained
- enough judgment to know when a feature needs a stricter interaction model, not another UI polish pass

#### What a hiring team is optimizing for in screening
Screening is probably looking for:
- shipped game UX, not just general product UX
- console/controller fluency
- evidence of systems thinking at scale
- proof you can work in a production environment with QA, engineering, and art
- a portfolio that shows flows, prototypes, and implementation-ready structure

They will also be filtering for whether you can fit a product-minded UX role, not an art or front-end engineering role. The line matters here.

#### Likely doubts unless proven otherwise
- The strongest public proof is older, so a screener may wonder how current your toolchain and workflows are.
- The JD explicitly calls for Figma component systems, but the public story does not foreground modern Figma mastery.
- The role is RPG/action-RPG adjacent; your background is close, but not a named Last Epoch-style match.
- They may want more explicit evidence of in-game shop and live-product iteration than the resume alone gives them.
- If they read too fast, they could type you as "game UX veteran" without immediately seeing the exact production-language fit they want.

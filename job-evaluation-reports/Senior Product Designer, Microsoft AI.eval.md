## PART 1 — What the role is really asking for

#### The implied asks
- Turn a broad AI capability set into one coherent, trustworthy product story.
- Make personality and emotional intelligence feel intentional rather than decorative.
- Move fast without losing quality, safety, or clarity.
- Be comfortable with ambiguity, changing priorities, and rapid iteration.
- Bring real prototyping strength so the team can test behavior early.
- Assume the `experience prompting LLMs` line is a preferred signal, not the whole gate; the screen is still about product design judgment, consumer polish, and trust.

#### What "great" looks like for this role
- A designer who can define the interaction model for a consumer AI companion across multiple skills or surfaces.
- Someone who can keep the product feel simple, warm, and dependable while the underlying model behavior changes.
- A craftsperson who can prototype, test, and refine quickly, then carry the work through implementation.
- A senior IC who can raise the team’s bar with concrete artifacts, not just opinions.
- A designer who can combine qualitative insight, metrics, and engineering constraints into one usable direction.

#### What a hiring team is optimizing for in screening
- Fast proof that this is a senior product designer, not only a visual designer.
- Evidence of consumer-grade ship experience, not just concept work.
- Signs that the candidate can collaborate cleanly with engineering and research.
- A public story that feels close enough to AI product work to be credible.
- Trust that the person can work in a fast-moving environment without becoming sloppy.

#### Likely doubts unless proven otherwise
- The resume does not scream “consumer AI product designer” on first pass.
- The strongest public proof is older and game-heavy, so some screeners may need help seeing the Microsoft AI transfer.
- There is no explicit public LLM feature-ownership story in the submittable package.

## PART 1 — What the role is really asking for

#### The explicit asks
- Be the first UX designer and own complex UX problems without much oversight.
- Bring substantial game UX experience, especially in a large AAA co-development setting.
- Push best practices, extend existing UX patterns, and take concepts through wireframes and prototyping into scalable UI.
- Collaborate closely with leadership, production, and an embedded AAA client team.
- Establish standards, documentation, and working methods for a new discipline inside the studio.

#### The implied asks
- This is a practice-building role as much as a screen-design role: the candidate has to define the lane, not just fill it.
- The team wants someone who can influence without authority and stay useful inside a large organization.
- The posting reads like a mix of must-haves and wishlist items, which is common in games/AAA roles; I am treating Unreal/UMG, diegetic UI, and motion-based work as likely important signals, but not assuming every tool mention is an absolute gate.
- The first UX hire will probably be judged on whether they can create structure quickly while still being collaborative and light enough for a fast-moving project.

#### What "great" looks like for this role
- A senior game UX lead who can turn ambiguity into reviewable artifacts and then into shippable UI.
- Someone who can keep standards, critique, and documentation moving without slowing production.
- A designer who can work comfortably with art, engineering, production, and another UX team.
- A person whose portfolio makes it obvious that they have led game UI systems, not just contributed to isolated screens.

#### What a hiring team is optimizing for in screening
- Fast confirmation of real game UX leadership.
- Evidence of AAA production maturity, not just visual taste.
- Proof that the candidate can structure messy requirements into usable interaction design.
- Signs of collaboration, coaching, and practical delivery under constraints.

#### Likely doubts unless proven otherwise
- Whether the candidate has explicit Unreal / UMG / material-motion production skill, not just adjacent prototype work.
- Whether the candidate’s strongest proof is old enough to feel current for a new lead role.
- Whether the candidate can fit a co-development environment where another UX team already exists.
- Whether the candidate is broad enough to contribute across disciplines without drifting away from UX ownership.

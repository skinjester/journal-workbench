### PART 1 — What the role is really asking for

#### The explicit asks

- Rapidly design complex wireframes with leads and directors.
- Handle diegetic UI and 3D layouts, not just flat menus.
- Iterate on flows and interaction design while improving the overall player experience.
- Own UX through approvals, iteration, and cross-functional implementation.
- Bring strong visual hierarchy, contrast, and polish to game UI.
- Modify Blueprints and game data when needed to validate design.
- Build documentation that communicates UX vision clearly.
- Learn new tools proactively and mentor others.

#### The implied asks

- Be comfortable owning a problem from concept through implementation in a game engine.
- Read as a senior partner who can move between UX, visual design, engineering, and production.
- Make tradeoffs quickly in ambiguous production spaces.
- Know when the posting is asking for a must-have versus a wishlist item; this reads like a game/AAA JD with some broad language mixed in.
- Be able to explain and defend design decisions in a way that survives a skeptical lead or director review.

#### What "great" looks like for this role

- A designer who can make a complicated player flow feel obvious on first contact.
- Someone who can turn vague feature intent into a prototype the team can react to quickly.
- Strong enough systems thinking to keep the UI coherent across surfaces, states, and production changes.
- Enough engine fluency to collaborate with implementation without hand-waving.
- Clear written and verbal communication, especially when UX needs to be justified across disciplines.

#### What a hiring team is optimizing for in screening

- Immediate relevance to shipped game UX, ideally on console or live-service systems.
- Evidence of in-engine or production-adjacent implementation, not just concept work.
- A portfolio that shows clear hierarchy, interaction clarity, and design judgment.
- Senior-level communication that can hold up in a team with leads, directors, engineering, and production.
- Confidence that the candidate can work in the realities of game development rather than only in abstract UX terms.

#### Likely doubts unless proven otherwise

- Whether the candidate is current enough on modern game-engine workflows and production cadence.
- Whether the public story is more systems/menu UX than diegetic or spatial game UX.
- Whether the strongest examples are old enough to feel like background rather than current operating mode.
- Whether the candidate can do the visual/3D side of the role as deeply as the systems side.
- Whether the resume alone makes the role fit obvious fast, or whether the screener has to work too hard to connect the dots.

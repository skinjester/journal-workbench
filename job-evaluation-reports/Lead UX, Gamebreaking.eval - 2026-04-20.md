### PART 1 — What the role is really asking for

#### The explicit asks

This is a lead-level game UX role for someone who can work with low supervision inside a co-dev setup, design complex game UX quickly, and raise team standards. The posting is not asking for generic product UX. It is asking for someone already legible as a games UX lead with AAA collaboration habits, controller-aware interaction judgment, and enough craft range to move from wireframes through prototype thinking into scalable UI.

Harder requirements on paper:

- Many years in games, not just adjacent interactive work
- Strong collaboration and feedback skills
- Leadership and coaching ability
- Unreal experience, specifically `UMG` and material-based motion design
- Deep understanding of diegetic UI and 3D layouts
- Ability to document standards and methods

#### The implied asks

Because this person would be the first UX designer at Gamebreaking, the real ask is part embedded lead, part discipline founder. They want someone who can enter an existing AAA production, earn trust fast, critique and extend patterns without creating churn, and quietly impose order on messy UI/UX surfaces.

The co-dev context matters. A hiring team here is likely screening for someone who can influence without formal authority, survive partial ownership, and communicate design intent clearly to external partners, internal production, and implementation teams.

#### What "great" looks like for this role

Great for this role is not just "good at UX." It is someone who can point to shipped or near-shipped game UX where they:

- translated messy or PC-first systems into controller-native flows
- set reusable patterns or standards other designers/engineers used
- handled ambiguity without waiting for permission
- prototyped enough to de-risk interaction decisions
- can talk credibly about spatial UI, 3D placement, or diegetic/in-world interaction choices

The ideal profile is a visibly current AAA or adjacent-to-AAA game UX lead who also has direct Unreal receipts and can coach others.

#### What a hiring team is optimizing for in screening

- Immediate recognition as a game UX designer, not a broad product generalist
- Evidence of seniority without management fluff
- Confidence that the candidate can work independently inside another studio's pipeline
- Proof of systems thinking, documentation, and standards-setting
- Low risk on engine/tool fit, especially Unreal-specific workflows

#### Likely doubts unless proven otherwise

- The application package does not directly prove `UMG` or material-based motion design.
- Diegetic UI / 3D layout understanding is present by implication and adjacent artifacts, but not named strongly enough to count as clean proof.
- A lot of the strongest AAA receipts are older than the hiring team may prefer for a 2026 lead hire.
- The candidate location shown on the resumes is `San Francisco, CA`, while the posting says the role is open to candidates living and working in Canada and Latin America. Unless there is separate eligibility context, this is a real screening risk.

## PART 1 — What the role is really asking for

#### The explicit asks

- Own lead UX direction for Call of Duty: Mobile, not just screen execution.
- Turn player data, research, and competitor analysis into flows, prototypes, and high-level design direction.
- Partner tightly with Product Management to identify the UX problems worth solving.
- Align with Tencent on direction so detailed UI can be implemented cleanly by another team.
- Evaluate post-launch performance and use that feedback to guide iteration.
- Establish standards and process for a team that is doing dedicated UX for the first time.

#### The implied asks

- Be the person who defines the UX function, not just contributes to it.
- Know how to operate in a mobile free-to-play environment where monetization, retention, and clarity all compete.
- Be comfortable with messy input: player pain points, live data, competitor patterns, and incomplete requirements.
- Make clear decisions fast enough that product and implementation teams can move without constant re-litigation.
- Be credible enough that a studio will trust you with the first dedicated UX seat on the team.
- Handle onsite expectations in Santa Monica and time-zone friction with China without turning it into process theater.

#### What "great" looks like for this role

- A lead who can reduce a complex mobile game down to a small number of high-value UX problems.
- Someone who can turn insight into a tangible concept, then keep sharpening it after launch.
- A collaborator who makes PMs, researchers, and implementation partners better aligned, not more confused.
- A designer who establishes standards that survive handoff, not just polished artifacts that die in review.
- Enough mobile intuition to know what should be changed versus what should be left alone.

#### What a hiring team is optimizing for in screening

- Fast bucketability as a real senior UX lead for games.
- Proof that the candidate has solved player-facing system problems before.
- Evidence of prototyping, iteration, and cross-functional influence.
- Confidence that the candidate can define the role, not wait to be told what it is.
- A package that looks like it can survive a live-service mobile environment, even if the title is unique.

#### Likely doubts unless proven otherwise

- No obvious mobile free-to-play shipping proof.
- No obvious recent CODM-scale live-service proof.
- No public evidence of deep analytics or experimentation on a mobile game product.
- Leadership is visible, but mostly as influence and standards, not formal people management.
- The strongest proof is older than ideal, which makes freshness the real risk.

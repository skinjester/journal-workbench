## PART 1 — What the role is really asking for
#### The explicit asks
- Lead UX strategy and execution for complex enterprise applications.
- Define workflows, layouts, interactions, and data visualizations.
- Conduct research, synthesize findings, and turn them into decisions.
- Work client-side with stakeholders and present design direction clearly.
- Mentor other designers and partner tightly with engineering.
- Bring 10+ years of experience in interaction-heavy, data-rich UX.

#### The implied asks
- Be credible in a client-facing environment where decisions are negotiated, not just designed.
- Be comfortable with ambiguity, scope shifting, and balancing business, technical, and user constraints.
- Read as a senior systems thinker who can move from discovery to implementation without losing the thread.
- Understand that in financial services, trust, legibility, and operational clarity are core UX concerns.
- Be able to influence without relying on a pure visual-design identity.

#### What "great" looks like for this role
- A principal-level UX lead who can walk into a messy financial workflow, frame the problem, and make it legible fast.
- Someone who can run research, extract patterns, and turn them into implementable flows without over-abstracting the problem.
- A designer who can hold their own with clients, PMs, and engineers while keeping the work grounded.
- Evidence of repeated success on complex, data-driven systems rather than one-off concept work.

#### What a hiring team is optimizing for in screening
- A fast bucket for senior enterprise UX leadership.
- Proof that the candidate can handle complex workflows, not just polished screens.
- Signals that the candidate can collaborate with client stakeholders and engineering under constraints.
- Enough domain adjacency to financial or operational products to reduce ramp risk.

#### Likely doubts unless proven otherwise
- The public work is much more game-heavy than finance-heavy.
- Asset management, trading, and institutional investment workflows are not directly shown.
- The public evidence for formal user research is lighter than the evidence for systems design and prototyping.
- The Boston on-site expectation may matter as a practical filter; I am treating that as a real screening constraint, not a soft preference.

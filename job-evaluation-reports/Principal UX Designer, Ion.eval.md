## PART 1 — What the role is really asking for

#### The explicit asks
This is a principal-level UX role for complex financial and enterprise applications. The job wants someone who can lead UX strategy, define workflows and information structure, research with users, collaborate with clients, and drive implementation with product and engineering.

#### The implied asks
The real ask is not "make screens." It is to reduce ambiguity in a high-stakes client environment, turn messy institutional workflows into legible interaction systems, and defend design decisions under scrutiny.

The role also implicitly wants:
- data-heavy UX judgment
- comfort presenting to stakeholders and clients
- cross-functional influence without direct authority
- enough systems thinking to handle desktop, web, and mobile together
- enough domain empathy to learn finance and trading workflows quickly
- willingness to be on-site in Boston most of the week and travel as needed

#### What "great" looks like for this role
Great here means a designer who can own a problem space, not just execute tickets. The person should be able to:
- frame the UX problem before the team starts drawing
- map workflows, states, exceptions, and data relationships
- translate research into a design direction people will actually ship
- make complex systems feel calm and coherent
- speak clearly to clients, PMs, engineers, and visual designers
- mentor other designers while still being hands-on enough to unblock the work

#### What a hiring team is optimizing for in screening
Screening is probably looking for three things:
- proof you can survive complexity
- proof you can communicate and influence
- proof you will not need to be taught basic UX strategy or design-systems thinking

For this specific JD, the team will also filter for whether you can bridge into financial or enterprise workflows without looking like a consumer/game-only specialist.

#### Likely doubts unless proven otherwise
- No obvious finance, trading, asset-management, or institutional-investor background
- No public case study that looks like a regulated enterprise desktop or trading workflow
- Strongest proof is older and retrospective, so a screener may wonder how current the skill set is
- San Francisco location does not obviously align with a Boston on-site client role
- The portfolio is strong, but some of the framing is game-heavy or creative-tech-heavy rather than finance-heavy

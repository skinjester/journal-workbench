### PART 1 — What the role is really asking for

#### The explicit asks
- Own the end-to-end UX for a biometric verification ecosystem spanning the scanned participant and the operator.
- Design across physical, digital, audio, lighting, and haptic touchpoints.
- Plan and conduct research with participants, operators, and deployment partners.
- Turn research into requirements, prototypes, service flows, and design guidelines.
- Lead new product introduction and sustain the experience after launch in the field.

#### The implied asks
- Make a trust-sensitive system feel legible fast, with low confusion and low error recovery cost.
- Translate industrial design and hardware constraints into usable flows rather than treating UX as a screen-only layer.
- Work across a lot of moving parts: device, software, operations, manufacturing, support, and leadership.
- Keep the experience consistent across regions, deployment contexts, and user types.
- Be comfortable making calls in ambiguity when the product is still evolving.

#### What "great" looks like for this role
- A candidate who can connect the participant journey, operator journey, and device behavior into one coherent service.
- Strong proof of design systems thinking, prototyping, and cross-functional alignment.
- A portfolio that shows physical/digital interaction, not just app screens.
- Evidence of research-driven iteration and shipping under real constraints.
- Enough operational maturity to think about reliability, field feedback, and sustainment, not just launch-day polish.

#### What a hiring team is optimizing for in screening
- Seniority that maps to a lead-level systems thinker, not a narrow screen designer.
- Fast confidence that the candidate can handle hardware-adjacent complexity without getting lost in aesthetics alone.
- Clear signals that the person can work with ID, engineering, research, and ops without needing a translator.
- A package that reads as device/product UX, not as a game-only or pure visual-design profile.

#### Likely doubts unless proven otherwise
- No direct public proof of biometric or identity-verification UX.
- No obvious operator-console, manufacturing, or field-sustainment case study on the surface.
- The public story leans game/platform-heavy unless the portfolio is read carefully.
- Research exists, but the public evidence is more prototype- and system-driven than formal research-ops driven.
- Audio, haptics, and environmental cues are mentioned in the JD, but the public materials do not yet make those a central specialty.

#### Assumption check
- I am treating the hardware, operator, and sustainment language as real work, not decorative boilerplate.
- I am also treating the sensory touchpoint language as important, but not necessarily the single hardest bar unless the team emphasizes it in interviews.

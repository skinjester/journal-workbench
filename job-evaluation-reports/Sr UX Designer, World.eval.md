## PART 1 — What the role is really asking for
#### The explicit asks
- Own the end-to-end experience for a biometric verification ecosystem.
- Design for both the participant and the operator, not one user only.
- Span physical, digital, and sensory touchpoints: device, UI, cues, environment, and instructions.
- Conduct research with participants, operators, and deployment partners, then translate it into requirements and prototypes.
- Lead UX from concept through launch, then keep improving the experience in the field.
- Work tightly with industrial design, hardware engineering, software, research, operations, and leadership.

#### The implied asks
- Translate a trust-sensitive system into something people can use without anxiety or confusion.
- Make a complex hardware-plus-software product feel legible in seconds.
- Balance privacy, reliability, compliance, and usability at global scale.
- Be comfortable with ambiguity, iteration, and partial information.
- Protect design intent through manufacturing, launch, and sustaining phases.

#### What "great" looks like for this role
- A designer who can make a multi-surface ecosystem feel coherent instead of stitched together.
- Someone who can bridge product UX, industrial design, operations, and engineering without getting lost in any one lane.
- Strong research synthesis and crisp decision-making under uncertainty.
- Enough prototyping fluency to prove behavior early, before build or tooling debt hardens.
- Practical judgment about what the experience needs to say, show, and feel across participant and operator contexts.

#### What a hiring team is optimizing for in screening
- Can this person handle a trust-and-verification problem, not just a pretty interface problem?
- Can they work with hardware and field realities without turning UX into an abstract layer?
- Do they have proof of systems thinking across surfaces, not only one surface type?
- Can they communicate clearly with industrial design, engineering, and operations?
- Do they read as someone who can lead messy early work and keep it coherent through launch?

#### Likely doubts unless proven otherwise
- The public package is much stronger on games and systems UX than on biometric hardware or industrial design.
- The site does not directly prove audio, haptics, lighting cues, or operator-console ownership.
- Most of the clearest proof is older than the role’s current context.
- The strongest portfolio work shows adjacent physical and spatial thinking, but not the exact World product stack.
- A reviewer could bucket the candidate as "veteran systems/game UX" unless the story is framed carefully.
